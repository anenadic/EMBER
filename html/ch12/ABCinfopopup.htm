ABC transporters are membrane proteins defined by a highly conserved domain, the ATP binding cassette (ABC). Powered by ATP hydrolysis, different ABC transporters transport a wide range of substrates, for example sugars, amino acids, hormones, mating factors, inorganic ions and xenobiotic substances. Typically, two transmembrane domains (TMDs) and two nucleotide binding domains (NBDs) are expressed. Hydrophobicity plots of a TMD often predict 6 TM helices, but there are notable exceptions such as MRP and TAP which have extra predicted TM helices.

Cartoon of the domain structure of an ABC transporter. 2 transmembrane domains (TMDs) and 2 nucleotide binding domains (NBDs). The latter domains, though cytosolic, are attached to the TMDs at the membrane, and drive the transport of the substrate through ATP hydrolysis.

Though ABC transporters are made up of four domains, these are often expressed as separate polypeptide chains. For example MDR, MRP and CFTR proteins are functional as a monomer and are each encoded by a single gene, but TAP is functional as a heterodimer of TAP1+2 and is accordingly encoded by two genes. Other than the canonical 2 TMDs + 2 NBDs structure, ABC transporters can have a homodimer of one NBD attached to the TMDs rather than two different NBDs.
